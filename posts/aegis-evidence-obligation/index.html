AEGIS: typed evidence and obligations for AI-native enterprise architecture

Why static architecture diagrams cannot govern AI systems, and a metamodel that makes evidence, obligations, and runtime signals first-class objects with a regulatory crosswalk.

Enterprise architecture is the discipline that aligns an organisation’s business strategy with its information assets, applications, and technology. Two open standards govern the field. TOGAF defines a lifecycle. ArchiMate defines a modelling language. Both assume systems that behave predictably. Both assume governance happens in periodic review meetings. Both assume an architecture document stays accurate for months after it is written.

AI-native systems break these assumptions. A deployed language model produces probabilistic outputs. An autonomous agent calls tools in situations it has not seen during testing. A retrieval-augmented pipeline changes its effective knowledge base every time new documents are ingested. When such a system decides on credit, hiring, or infrastructure configuration, a static diagram cannot tell you whether the system still meets its regulatory obligations. That question needs continuous, machine-checkable governance.

The regulatory pressure is concrete. The EU AI Act entered into force in August 2024. Its high-risk requirements become enforceable from August 2026 (European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2024). ISO/IEC 42001:2023 requires a traceable evidence index that links each obligation to specific processes and artefacts. NIST AI RMF names traceability as a core property of trustworthy AI. Current enterprise architecture tools do not produce conformity evidence at this level of detail or at this cadence.

What AEGIS adds

AEGIS is a typed ontology and lifecycle framework. It sits at the enterprise architecture layer above existing artefacts. The name stands for Architecture with Evidence, Governance, Intent, and Safety. The paper makes three contributions.

  1. An evidence-obligation metamodel. Seven new typed entities become first-class architectural objects. They connect to existing entities through seven typed relations. AEGIS profiles ArchiMate Next and extends W3C PROV-O, so existing models need no re-modelling.
  2. A continuous conformance lifecycle. The document-centric TOGAF lifecycle is replaced by a six-phase loop called IMVDOA.
  3. A regulatory crosswalk. All 38 ISO/IEC 42001 controls and the key EU AI Act articles for high-risk systems map to obligation types.

Recent work supplies useful artefacts at lower layers. Policy Cards encode per-agent obligations in machine-readable form (Mavračić 2025). AI Bills of Materials track model provenance. Attestable Audits cryptographically verify benchmark claims (Schnabl et al. 2025). LLM Audit Trails log lifecycle events (Ojewale et al. 2026). Each of these operates in isolation. A Policy Card governs one deployed agent. It does not know which architectural service the agent implements, which data asset it accesses, or which regulatory obligation that access is subject to. AEGIS connects agent-layer engineering to enterprise-level regulatory expectation.

The seven entity types

AEGIS defines seven typed entities as OWL2 classes, each aligned with PROV-O.

  • Evidence is a time-stamped, provenance-carrying artefact that attests a property of an architectural entity. Every instance carries an issuer, an issue time, an expiry time, a status in {valid, expired, superseded, revoked}, and a strength in [0,1].
  • Obligation is a machine-checkable normative requirement on an architectural entity. Each carries a deontic modality in {must, must_not, should, may}, a trigger condition expressed in SHACL or OPA Rego, the evidence it requires, a regulatory source, and a priority.
  • Policy is a named, versioned collection of obligations. It is the enterprise-level counterpart to an agent-level Policy Card.
  • Risk is a structured risk entry aligned with ISO 31000.
  • RuntimeSignal is an event emitted by a deployed system. Once linked to an architectural entity and an obligation it is promoted to an Evidence object.
  • Exception is a time-bounded, machine-readable dispensation from an obligation, recording who granted it and why.
  • ArchitectureDecisionRecord extends the architecture decision record concept and is machine-queryable.

The closure rule

The core inference is the evidence-obligation closure. Write \text{constrained\_by}(e,o) when architectural entity e is constrained by obligation o, and \text{verified\_by}(o,v) when evidence v verifies o. The rule is

\text{constrained\_by}(e,o) \;\wedge\; \neg\,\exists v\,\big(\text{verified\_by}(o,v) \wedge v.\text{status}=\text{valid}\big) \;\Rightarrow\; \text{violates}(e,o). \tag{1}

In words, an entity constrained by an obligation violates that obligation when no valid evidence verifies it. Equation 1 is evaluated continuously as part of the runtime loop.

A worked example shows how a violation appears over time. Service S1 is constrained by obligation O1, “PII must not flow to an external endpoint unless a redaction proof exists”, sourced from EU AI Act Article 10. Evidence E1 verifies O1 with an expiry of 2026-07-15. On 16 July 2026 the status of E1 becomes expired. The closure rule fires. S1 now violates O1. The conformance loop raises a runtime signal and escalates it to the responsible actor. No human re-review was needed to detect this.

The IMVDOA lifecycle

AEGIS replaces the linear TOGAF lifecycle with a continuous six-phase loop: Intent, Model, Verify, Deploy, Observe, Adapt.

  • Intent derives typed obligations from regulatory sources, organisational policies, and stakeholder constraints.
  • Model attaches policies, AI bills of materials, and obligations to every agent, service, and data asset.
  • Verify runs SHACL shape checks for structural completeness and a Defeasible Deontic Logic prover for obligation satisfiability. Failures block deployment.
  • Deploy registers a deployment event in the audit trail and activates fitness functions.
  • Observe consumes enforcement events, audit-trail entries, and audit results as runtime signals, then promotes verified signals to evidence.
  • Adapt fires when the per-entity conformance score falls below a threshold.

The conformance score for entity e is the fraction of its mandatory obligations that currently hold valid evidence.

\kappa(e) = \frac{\big|\{\, o : \text{constrained\_by}(e,o),\; o.\text{deontic}=\text{must},\; \exists v\,(\text{verified\_by}(o,v) \wedge v.\text{status}=\text{valid}) \,\}\big|}{\big|\{\, o : \text{constrained\_by}(e,o),\; o.\text{deontic}=\text{must} \,\}\big|}. \tag{2}

Adapt re-runs the Intent, Model, and Verify sub-cycle for the affected scope.

What the framework can express

The paper gives three expressiveness constructions. Each is a structured demonstration that the metamodel can represent a class of governance artefact or detect a class of failure by logical inference alone. No deployed system is measured.

The first maps all 38 ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A controls to typed obligations through the conformity triple “Control to Test to Evidence”, expressed as machine-queryable SPARQL over the obligation graph.

The second detects three classes of obligation drift by the closure rule. Evidence can expire without re-verification. A Policy Card can be updated at the agent layer while its enterprise-level policy is not. A regulatory update can supersede the source an obligation points to. ArchiMate models and code-level fitness functions detect none of these.

The third lifts agent-level Policy Cards into an enterprise-level conformance graph and exposes conflicts between cards that agent-layer enforcement cannot see.

Limits

The three constructions are expressiveness demonstrations. They do not report measurements from a deployed system. The crosswalk covers the EU AI Act and ISO/IEC 42001. Extension to other regimes such as GDPR or FDA AI/ML guidance needs additional tables. The Defeasible Deontic Logic prover has polynomial complexity and has not been profiled at enterprise scale. A full implementation covering the OWL2 ontology, the SHACL shapes, the SPARQL closure queries, and a Policy Card lifting tool is the primary target for future work. Empirical evaluation against existing toolchains requires that implementation first.
